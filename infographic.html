The Truth About Coffee in India.

An objective analysis of the domestic coffee landscape, uncovering the structural issues of purity, the prevalence of fillers, and the widening gap between commodity and specialty grades.

Analysis 01

The Adulteration Crisis

In the high-volume domestic market, the purity of coffee is frequently compromised to reduce manufacturing costs. Fillers are not just additives; they are industrial shortcuts that dilute the sensory profile of the bean.

Chicory Substitution

The most common additive, often making up to 40% of commercial blends. Derived from roots, it provides a heavy, bitter body but lacks any caffeine or complex acidity.

Roasted Corn & Grains

Industrial fillers used in lower-tier instant coffees to add bulk. These starches carbonize during roasting, masking the taste of moldy or defective beans.

Did you know?

FSSAI allows up to 49% chicory in 'Coffee-Chicory Mixtures', but many unbranded sellers exceed this limit illegally.

Label Check

Pure coffee must state "100% Arabica" or "100% Robusta". Avoid ambiguous terms like "Freshly Ground Blend" without percentage disclosures.

The Cold Water Test

Sprinkle grounds onto cold water. Pure coffee floats; chicory and additives contain sugars that absorb water instantly and sink, leaving a brown trail.

The Texture Rub

Rub coffee between your fingers. Pure coffee is dry and slightly gritty. Adulterated coffee feels greasy or sticky due to the higher starch and sugar content.

The Quality Gap

Commodity Grade 82%

Industrial mass-market coffee with high defect counts and standard commercial processing.

Specialty Grade 18%

SCA rated (80+) coffee, ethically sourced, fully traceable, and chemically pure.

Market Insight
